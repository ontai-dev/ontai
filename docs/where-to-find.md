# Where to Find Operator Profiles
The specific profile you need is a platform engineer or SRE who operates on-premises Kubernetes in a regulated or operationally complex environment, has direct experience with configuration drift incidents, and has felt the pressure of compliance audits or post-mortem archaeology. This person exists in concentrated form in specific communities.
## Kubernetes SIG meetings and CNCF Slack. 
The CNCF Slack has channels specifically for platform engineering, SRE, and Kubernetes operators. The people in these channels are not cloud architects reading about Kubernetes. They are operators running it. The sig-platform and sig-multicluster channels have the right density of on-premises operators. The Kubernetes Slack at kubernetes.slack.com has over 100,000 members and the on-premises operations communities within it are active.
## KubeCon hallway track. 
The talks at KubeCon are often the wrong place to find this profile because they skew toward vendors and cloud-native enthusiasts. The hallway track and the platform engineering day events attached to KubeCon are where on-premises operators congregate. If there is a KubeCon near you this year, the Platform Engineering Day co-located event is specifically the right room.
## Fintech and banking infrastructure communities. 
Given that the ONT thesis uses fintech as its primary domain example, the FinTech Open Source Foundation (FINOS) community is a direct match. FINOS specifically serves financial services organizations running regulated infrastructure on open source platforms. Their Slack and GitHub discussions have platform engineers operating exactly the CBS-class systems the thesis describes.
## Local Kubernetes and platform engineering meetups. 
You have already presented ONT at a meetup. That is the right format. The three operators you need for validation do not have to be strangers. They can be people in the same city who operate similar infrastructure. The meetup community is accessible, willing to give honest feedback, and has the right operational context.
## LinkedIn with a specific search pattern. 
Search for "Platform Engineer" or "SRE" combined with "on-premises" or "bare-metal" and any regulated industry term. People who describe their work as operating Kubernetes on bare metal in fintech, healthcare, or telecoms are the exact profile. A direct message explaining that you have built something addressing a specific operational pain and asking for thirty minutes of honest feedback has a reasonable response rate when the pain description is precise. The pain description from Part One is precise enough to use as the outreach message.
## Talos Linux community. 
Since ONT is built on Talos Linux, the Talos Slack and GitHub discussions already contain people who have made a deliberate choice to operate immutable, declarative Kubernetes infrastructure. This community has a high density of the right profile and a shared technical foundation that makes ONT immediately legible without lengthy context-setting.
The key is the outreach framing. You are not asking for product feedback. You are asking whether the problem description in Part One matches their experience. That is a much easier yes than "please evaluate my platform." If someone reads Part One and says "yes, that is exactly the pain I have," you have found your profile. The conversation that follows is the validation.
